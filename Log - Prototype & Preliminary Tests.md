Log 2 \- 3/7/2026

	Performed preliminary circuit test using 3d printed flywheel and 20V Craftsman drill. The advertised rotational speed of the drill was 400 RPMs in low gear and 1500 RPM in high gear. Upon testing, readings fluctuated between 372 and 375 on low gear and 1420 and 1463 on high gear.  
	Testing concluded, at most, a 7 percent error for low gear and a 5.33 percent error for high gear. While this is significantly higher than the targeted 2% percent error, it is worth noting that both readings were below the advertised speed of the drill. The advertised speed is likely only achievable under low or zero load, and the flywheel was slightly unbalanced and also contributes a fair amount of air resistance, meaning that the reading is likely closer than it initially appears.  
	One consideration worth looking into is that the RPM readings fluctuated between two constant numbers. Instead of hovering around 1440, the reading bounced between exactly 1420 and exactly 1463\. My primary theory is that this is due to the use of the millis() function in the arduino board’s programming, however further testing will confirm whether this is the case.  
	Some quick math confirms that this is the most likely cause, as with the formula of ((milliseconds / 1000)/rotation)\-1 \* 60, which is used in the code to calculate RPM, results in a value of 1463.4 RPM using 41 milliseconds and 1428.6 RPM using 42 milliseconds. Upon closer inspection of slowed video footage, the display does in fact show 1428 rather than 1420\. Given that the math lines up with the test data, it is confirmed that the cause of the issue is the use of the millis function. This means that the code will need restructured around the use of micros(), which carries some technical limitations that must be addressed, primarily the 71-minute overflow timer which may or may not cause issues in sustained use.  
	In particular, the 71-minute timer is not likely to cause problems, as it will only affect one single reading after 71 minutes of continuous running. Most likely, I will address the problem by adding an if-else statement that keeps RPMs the same if the most recent micros() is lower than the previous micros, indicating an overflow. The code should then keep RPMs identical, as the issue will only last for one single revolution cycle, and most likely won’t even be visible on the rather limited LCD screen. An unsigned long must be used to manage overflow errors and prevent negative time readings.  
	A final issue that is worth noting is that the LCD took nearly a full second to update the rpm readings

Log 3 \- 3/7/2026

	The drill flywheel was tested a second time. Unfortunately, at the first attempt to test it, the flywheel experienced a catastrophic mechanical failure and no data was able to be gathered. I repaired the flywheel using hot glue, however the flywheel became unbalanced, likely further reducing the effective speed of the drill and potentially risking further failure.  
	The test was performed after performing most of the modifications prescribed in log 2\. In particular, the overflow exception was not added, but will be integrated before further developing the prototype. Despite the flywheel issues, the testing was pleasantly surprising. As opposed to the original 370-373 reading, the new reading oscillated between 371 and 372 exactly, proving that the millis() function was causing the error. The reading of the drill in high gear was rather difficult to discern exactly, but it was possible to tell that it was moving in increments of 1 even at higher speeds. Another note is that these modifications drastically reduced the delay of the lcd, from nearly a second to barely noticeable levels. It is unclear why this is the case, and further research must be performed, but I will continue to keep in mind if the delay comes back and see if it can be attributed to anything other than the use of the millis() function.
